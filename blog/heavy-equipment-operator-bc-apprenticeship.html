Heavy Equipment Operator Apprenticeship in BC — How It Works

If you want to operate heavy equipment in BC and do it right — with a real ticket, real wages, and a pension that doesn't disappear when the job ends — you need to understand how the apprenticeship system works. There are two routes: the union path through IUOE Local 115, and the non-union path through SkilledTradesBC. Both end up in the same place. How you get there is different.

This is the honest breakdown of both, written for people who are actually trying to get into the trade — not just reading about it.

Route 1: IUOE Local 115 (Union Apprenticeship)

The International Union of Operating Engineers Local 115 is the primary union for heavy equipment operators in BC. If you're aiming for major civil projects — Site C, LNG, Trans Mountain-type work, highway construction — this is the path. The wages, benefits, and dispatch system are built around large-scale unionized contractors.

How to Apply

Apply through the Local 115 website at iuoe115.ca. The apprenticeship intake process opens periodically — it's not always accepting applications year-round. When it's open, you submit your application, pay the application fee, and wait to be called in for the aptitude test.

The aptitude test covers math, mechanical reasoning, and reading comprehension. Passing is mandatory — there's no workaround. You get a limited number of attempts, so prepare before you walk in. Our IUOE aptitude test practice guide and how to pass the IUOE test page will help.

Dispatch

Once you pass the aptitude test and are accepted into the apprenticeship, you get dispatched to work. Local 115 operates on a dispatch hall system — jobs are posted, and dispatchers send members out to fill positions. As an apprentice, you're generally at the bottom of the dispatch list. You take what's available, build hours, and move up.

This matters because your income during apprenticeship depends on how often you're dispatched. Active projects = more dispatches = faster progression. Slow construction markets mean slower hour accumulation.

Seat Time by Machine

The Local 115 apprenticeship is structured around hours on specific equipment. You need to log seat time across multiple machine types — you're not just an excavator operator, you're a heavy equipment operator. The program tracks your hours by category:

  • Excavators (tracked and wheeled)
  • Bulldozers / crawler tractors
  • Motor graders
  • Loaders (wheel and track)
  • Compaction equipment
  • Scrapers and specialty equipment (site-dependent)

Total program hours typically run in the 6,000–8,000 hour range depending on your equipment mix and the specific program level. Your dispatcher and the union hall will track your hours and flag when you're eligible to move to the next level.

Wage Scale

Union apprentice wages are a percentage of the journeyman rate, which is set by the collective agreement. As of recent agreements, Local 115 journeyman operators on major projects earn between $50–$65/hr base depending on equipment type and project type. Apprentice scale runs roughly:

  • First period: ~60% of journeyman rate
  • Second period: ~65–70%
  • Third period: ~75–80%
  • Final period: ~85%

On top of the hourly rate, union members earn pension contributions, benefits, and in many cases vacation pay — all paid by the employer. The pension is not optional. It stacks whether you're working or not (as long as you're working union hours).

Route 2: SkilledTradesBC (Non-Union)

SkilledTradesBC (formerly the Industry Training Authority) administers the provincial apprenticeship system for non-union employers. If you're working for a non-union contractor — a private earthworks company, a smaller civil outfit — this is the path to get a formal ticket.

How It Works

To register an apprenticeship through SkilledTradesBC, you need a sponsoring employer — a contractor who's willing to register you in the program and provide your on-the-job training. The employer and apprentice both register through the SkilledTradesBC portal.

The training has two components:

  • Industry-Based Training (IBT): On-the-job hours logged with your employer, supervised by a qualified journeyperson. This is the bulk of your training — you learn by doing, on real projects, with your employer counting your hours.
  • Technical Training: Classroom and shop training at a recognized trades institution (BCIT, trades schools). Typically done in blocks — you leave the job site for a few weeks per level to complete the technical component. Employers are required to allow this; some continue paying wages during technical training.

Provincial Certification

Completing the SkilledTradesBC apprenticeship earns you a BC Certificate of Qualification. You can then apply to add the Red Seal endorsement by writing the interprovincial exam — which makes your ticket portable across all Red Seal provinces.

How Long Does It Take?

Realistically: 2 to 4 years. The variation comes from how consistently you're working and how your hours accumulate.

  • Full-time union work on an active major project: can hit required hours in 2–3 years
  • Non-union seasonal work: 3–4 years is more typical, especially if you're laying off winters
  • Part-time or inconsistent dispatch: can stretch to 5+ years

There's no shortcut. The hours are the hours. The program is built around logged seat time, and the union or SkilledTradesBC will verify your records before advancing you.

What You End Up With

At the end of either path, you have:

  • A Journeyman Certificate of Qualification — recognized in BC and, with the Red Seal endorsement, across Canada
  • The ability to supervise apprentices yourself
  • Access to journeyman dispatch rates and full benefits (union path)
  • Documented proof of your skills — useful for every employer, every project, for the rest of your career
Honest Operator Advice

Get into the union if you can long-term — the pension is the reason. The hourly rate matters, but it's the pension that separates the operators who retire comfortable from the ones still grinding at 60. Non-union work pays well on some projects. But the union pension accrues your whole career, it's managed professionally, and it doesn't evaporate when a contractor folds. If Local 115 is accepting applications and you're serious about this trade, the aptitude test is the gate. Pass it. Everything else follows.
