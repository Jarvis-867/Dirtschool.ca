Your First Day as a Heavy Equipment Apprentice — What to Actually Expect

April 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  Dirt School

You passed the aptitude test. You're on the dispatch list. The call finally comes. Now what?

The first dispatch is both simpler and more intense than people expect. Here's what the first day and first week actually look like — no sugarcoating, no motivational poster version.

Before You Show Up

When dispatch calls, get the basics confirmed before you hang up: project name, site address or camp location, reporting time, who to ask for when you arrive, and what to bring. Some sites have strict PPE requirements — steel toes, hard hat, hi-vis. If you don't own these, get them before day one. Showing up without basic PPE is a red flag on the first day.

For camp jobs: find out if accommodation and meals are provided (they usually are) or if you need to arrange your own. Confirm travel arrangements — some projects provide a bus from a staging area, others you drive yourself. Get this sorted before you're trying to figure it out at 4am.

Bring: your union card, your driver's licence, any relevant certs (WHMIS, H2S Alive, first aid), and a change of work clothes. First impressions matter and showing up clean and prepared communicates that you take the work seriously.

The First Morning

Site orientation comes before you touch anything. Every site runs one — some are 30 minutes, some take most of the morning. Pay attention. The orientation covers muster points, safety zones, communication channels, site layout, and any site-specific hazards. You will be tested on none of it but you need to actually know it.

At the end of orientation you will typically be assigned to a journeyman operator or a foreman. This person is now your immediate supervisor for the foreseeable future. How this relationship starts sets the tone for how the season goes.

What Foremen Actually Want to See on Day One

This is the part nobody tells you clearly enough.

Show up ready to work, not ready to operate. As a new apprentice, your first job is probably not getting in the machine. It might be fuelling the machine. Or doing the walkaround inspection while the journeyman watches. Or riding along and watching. That is normal. That is not a demotion. That is how you learn a crew's standards before you start working to them.

Ask questions at the right time. During breaks, at lunch, or when the journeyman explicitly invites them. Not when the machine is running and somebody is trying to concentrate. Not when the foreman is on the radio. Good judgment about timing is a signal that you can read a job site.

Do the walkaround properly. When it is your turn, do it slowly and do it right. If a journeyman watches you rush a pre-op check, you have told them something about yourself. If they watch you do it methodically — fluids, belts, tracks, lights, everything — you have told them something much better.

Do not touch things without asking first. Every machine has quirks. Every crew has a way they do things. Ask before you adjust the seat, change a setting, or move an attachment. One question takes five seconds. Making an assumption and getting it wrong takes much longer to undo.

Be the last one at the machine at end of shift. Basic, but noticed. Do the post-op inspection. Put the machine to bed correctly. Shut everything down in the right order. You are building a reputation from day one.

Camp Life — The Real Version

If your first dispatch is a camp job — which it often is, especially in BC and Yukon — the lifestyle adjustment is real. Here's what it looks like:

Your room: A single in a camp trailer or modular building. Small, functional, private. Wifi varies — some camps have it in common areas, some have good signal, some barely. Starlink has changed this on newer remote jobs significantly.

Meals: Camp kitchen runs 24 hours on most large projects. The food is generally good — contractors know that bad food causes turnover. You will eat more than you do at home. That is fine, you are working harder than you do at home.

Downtime: After a 10-12 hour shift, most people eat and go to sleep. The gym (if the camp has one) and the common room TV get use. The guys who burn themselves out on alcohol or late nights in the first two weeks do not make it to month two. Pace yourself. It's a long season.

The social dynamic: Camp is a small community. Word gets around fast. Be reliable, be easy to work with, and stay out of drama. Your reputation on one project follows you to the next — this industry is smaller than it looks.

Your First Time in the Seat

When it comes — and it will come quickly on most projects — you will be nervous. That is normal. The machine feels bigger than it did in your head. The controls are right there but using them with someone watching feels different.

A few things that help:

  • Move slowly at first. Not lazily — deliberately. Smooth, controlled inputs. The journeyman watching you is not looking for speed. They are looking for control and awareness.
  • Talk through what you are doing. "I'm going to lower the bucket and push forward." It sounds unnecessary but it demonstrates you know what you are doing and why. It also gives the journeyman a chance to correct you before you do something wrong.
  • Keep your eyes moving. Ground crew, other machines, overhead lines, blind spots. You are responsible for everything in your swing radius. Develop that habit from the first day.
  • Stop when you are unsure. Park the machine, get out, look at the situation, then continue. Getting stuck or breaking something because you pushed through uncertainty is worse than taking a minute to think.

The Paperwork — Do Not Skip It

Two things every morning, every site, no exceptions:

Machine inspection log: Your walkaround, written down. Not a mental note — written down. Deficiencies go to the mechanic. If you find something and do not write it down, you are responsible for what happens after.

FLRA (Field Level Risk Assessment): Five minutes. What could go wrong today. Write it down, sign it. On regulated highway and mine sites, inspectors check these. Not filling it out can result in personal fines. Fill it out and mean it.

The Long Game

The first dispatch is just the start. The hours you accumulate this season go toward your apprenticeship. The reputation you build on this crew follows you to the next one. The relationship you build with this foreman can mean a direct call for next season instead of waiting at the bottom of the dispatch list.

Every journeyman operator you work under is someone who learned from operators before them. Pay attention to how they do things. Not everything they do will be the way you end up doing it — but you need to understand the reasoning behind their methods before you can develop your own.

"Show up on time. Do the walkaround right. Keep your mouth shut and your eyes open for the first two weeks. The rest takes care of itself."
