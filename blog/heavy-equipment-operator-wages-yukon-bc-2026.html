Heavy Equipment Operator Wages in BC and Yukon — The Real Numbers (2026)

April 2026  ·  6 min read  ·  Dirt School

Most wage discussions for heavy equipment operators either throw out a vague range or quote oil sands rates that don't apply to most operators. This is what BC and Yukon union operators actually make — seasonal reality included.

IUOE Local 115 — The Union That Covers BC and Yukon

If you're running equipment on a major highway, pipeline, mine, or infrastructure project in BC or Yukon, you're likely working under an IUOE Local 115 collective agreement. Local 115 is based in Maple Ridge and covers over 14,000 members.

Wages come from collective agreements negotiated between Local 115 and the contractors. Rates vary by project type — industrial vs. non-industrial — and by equipment classification.

Hourly Rates — What the Work Actually Pays

For highway construction and general civil work (non-industrial), journeyman rates run roughly:

  • General operator (grader, compactor, water truck): $42–48/hr
  • Excavator, large loader, dozer: $46–52/hr
  • Crane operators: $50–65/hr depending on tonnage

For industrial work (pipeline, mine sites, larger industrial contracts) rates are higher — comparable to Alberta non-oil-sands rates.

Add to base: vacation pay (typically 6%), holiday pay (4%), and benefits through the union trust fund.

The Real Income Math — Seasonal Reality

Here's what catches people who compare hourly rates without thinking about the hours.

A typical highway construction season in BC and Yukon runs roughly May through October — about 6 months. During that season, it's common to work:

  • 10–12 hour days
  • 6–7 days a week during peak pushing season
  • Camp-based jobs — you're on site, not commuting

"The hourly rate isn't the whole story. The hours are."

The Pension — The Part Nobody Talks About Enough

The IUOE pension is a defined benefit plan. Your employer contributes a set amount per hour worked into the pension trust fund on your behalf. You don't see it in your paycheque — it accumulates separately.

A career operator who works 25–30 seasons under the union retires with monthly pension income that most salaried workers can't match with standard RRSP contributions. The pension doesn't depend on stock market performance. It's a fixed benefit based on your hours and contribution history.

For young operators: this is the financial argument for the union card beyond the hourly rate. The compounding effect of employer pension contributions over a career is substantial.

Apprentice vs. Journeyman

As an apprentice you earn a percentage of journeyman rate — typically starting around 60–70% and progressing in steps as you accumulate hours and complete training levels. You're earning while learning, with no student debt.

The progression to journeyman depends on hours accumulated and training completed. Most operators reach journeyman within 4–5 seasons of consistent work.

Getting In — The Aptitude Test

The path to these wages runs through the Local 115 aptitude test. It's the gate between where you are and the dispatch list.

The test covers math, mechanical reasoning, reading comprehension, and spatial reasoning. Most experienced operators stumble on the math section — not because the content is hard, but because written fractions under test conditions feel different from field calculations. You get a limited number of attempts.
