Heavy Equipment Operator Log Book — What Should Be In It

April 2026  ·  7 min read  ·  Dirt School

Most operators don't keep a log book. Most operators also can't tell you exactly how many hours they put on a machine last season, or when the 500-hour service was done, or what they were producing on the day something broke.

A log book is your professional record. It takes five minutes at the end of each shift to fill out and pays for itself the first time you have a dispute about hours worked, a warranty claim on a repair, or an incident investigation.

1. Daily Work Log

The core of any operator log book. One page per shift. At a minimum, track:

  • Date, project name, site location
  • Equipment and unit number
  • Operator name and foreman
  • Start and end time, break time, regular hours, overtime hours
  • Tasks performed — what you actually did (grade road, excavate footing, load trucks, etc.) with quantities if you know them
  • Equipment hour meter — start and end reading
  • Site conditions — weather, ground conditions, temperature
  • Delays — what caused them, how long, who you reported to
  • Notes — anything unusual, safety concerns, equipment issues
  • Operator and foreman signatures

On payroll disputes: A signed daily log with your foreman's signature is your evidence. On camp jobs especially, hours get miscounted. Your log book is your backup.

2. Pre-Shift Inspection Log

A record of each daily walk-around. Not just "did it" — what you checked, whether it passed or failed, and any defects you found. The pre-shift inspection log is your legal protection if a machine has a defect that causes an incident after you flagged it.

Each sheet should cover:

  • Engine oil, coolant, hydraulics, fuel, DEF/AdBlue
  • Belts, hoses, battery
  • Lights, horn, backup alarm
  • Seat belt, ROPS, fire extinguisher
  • Windows, mirrors, steps and walkways
  • Undercarriage, tires, or tracks
  • Bucket/blade, attachment pins, ground engagement tools
  • Hydraulic lines and cylinders
  • Gauges and warning lights
  • Operator signature and time

3. Fuel and Hour Meter Log

Track every fuel fill — date, hour meter reading, litres or gallons, and fuel source. This lets you calculate fuel burn rate per hour. A sudden increase in fuel consumption is often the first sign of a mechanical problem before it becomes an expensive one.

Over a season, this data tells you your actual operating cost per hour, which feeds directly into better estimates on future jobs.

4. Maintenance and Repair Log

Every PM, repair, inspection, and warranty item — date, hour meter, what was done, parts replaced, who did it, and a sign-off. Type codes keep it clean: PM = preventive maintenance, R = repair, I = inspection, W = warranty.

This is the document your mechanic or dealer looks at when something goes wrong. Without it, they're guessing. With it, they can see the full service history.

5. Incident and Near-Miss Log

Document every incident and near-miss, however minor. Date, time, location, what happened, who was involved, whether it's WCB/WHS reportable. Signatures from both the operator and supervisor.

"A near-miss is a free lesson. Report it — don't hide it."

This isn't about blame. It's about fixing the system before the next one is a real incident.

6. Equipment Hours by Machine

A running total of hours per machine for the season — with service interval reminders built in. Track when the next 250-hr, 500-hr, and 1000-hr service is due. Proactive maintenance is cheaper than emergency repairs every time.

Reference Section

A good log book includes a quick reference section so you're not Googling conversions in the field:

  • Grade stake colour reference (orange = fill, blue = cut, green = grade)
  • Common slope ratios and percent grades
  • Swell and shrinkage factors by material
  • Unit conversions (cubic metres to yards, tonnes to tons, etc.)
  • Maintenance intervals reference
  • Standard hand signals

How Long Should a Log Book Last?

A typical construction season runs 5–7 months. An operator working 10-hour shifts needs roughly 90 daily log pages to get through a full season with room to spare. Weekly summaries, inspection sheets, fuel logs, and maintenance records add another 40–50 pages on top of that.

A complete log book for one operator for one season should be 120–150 pages.
